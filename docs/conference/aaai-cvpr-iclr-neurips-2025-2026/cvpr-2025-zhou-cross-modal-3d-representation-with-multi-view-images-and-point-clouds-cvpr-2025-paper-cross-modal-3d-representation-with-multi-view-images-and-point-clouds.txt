# Abstract 

The advancement of 3D understanding and representation is a crucial step for the next phase of autonomous driving, robotics, augmented and virtual reality, 3D gaming and 3D e-commerce products. However, existing 3D semantic repre-sentation research has primarily focused on point clouds to perceive 3D objects and scenes, overlooking the rich visual details offered by multi-view images, thereby limiting the po-tential of 3D semantic representation. This paper introduces OpenView, a novel representation method that integrates both point clouds and multi-view images to form a unified 3D representation. OpenView comprises a unique fusion framework, sequence-independent modeling, a cross-modal fusion encoder, and a progressive hard learning strategy. Our experiments demonstrate that OpenView outperforms the state-of-the-art by 11.5% and 5.5% on the R@1 metric for cross-modal retrieval and the Top-1 metric for zero-shot classification tasks, respectively. Furthermore, we showcase some applications of OpenView: 3D retrieval, 3D captioning and hierarchical data clustering, highlighting its generality in the field of 3D representation learning.

Additionally, 3D games comprise a large number of 3D models, and their development can benefit from automated 3D generation and retrieval. Furthermore, e-commerce prod-uct displays are transitioning from 2D images to 3D models, requiring 3D semantic understanding and representation. These real-world applications show the importance of re-search in 3D understanding. # 2. Related Work 

2.1. 3D Rendering based on Multi-View Images 

3D rendering based on multi-view images has been studied for a long time in the history of computer graphics [ 4, 22 ,46 , 47 , 50 ]. The basic approach is to render new views by interpolating pixel values from a given set of multi-view images. With the rise of deep learning, some studies [ 3,8, 39 , 57 , 58 ] start to combine deep learning techniques to predict depth information [ 3, 8 , 39 ] and image features [ 57 , 58 ] to facilitate pixel matching between different views. Recently, there have emerged many studies focusing on 3D generation [ 29 , 31 – 33 ] and 3D reconstruction [ 13 , 20 , 25 ,73 ] from multi-view or single-view images. For instance, SparseFusion [ 73 ] is based on a view-conditioned latent diffusion model with few views as input and a 3D scene representation as output, which can render novel views. The above studies demonstrate that multi-view images contain rich 3D visual information and reveal the feasibility of 3D representation learning based on multi-view images. 

2.2. Single-Modal 3D Representation Learning 

Learning 3D Representations from Point Clouds. This line of methods focuses on the self-supervised pretrain-ing of point clouds using pretext tasks, without involving multimodal alignment. These pretext tasks include self-reconstruction [ 11 , 44 , 55 ], mask point modeling [ 38 , 70 ], and contrastive learning [ 45 , 63 , 71 ]. Further, beyond the self-reconstruction task, Rao et al. [ 44 ] performed self-supervised training by mapping global features and different levels of local features to the same feature space. Besides, Yu et al. [ 70 ] utilized a discrete variational auto-encoder to generate discrete point tokens, mask local point patches, and predict discrete point tokens during the training. Their down-stream tasks include 3D classification, 3D pose estimation [ 66 ], etc. However, the absence of multimodal alignment hindered its applicability to cross-modal downstream tasks, such as text-based 3D retrieval and 3D captioning. 

Learning 3D Representations from Images. This line of methods [ 18 , 48 , 60 ] learns a compact latent code that encodes the texture, geometry, and pose details of the 3D shape. The typical downstream task is 3D reconstruction using the compact latent code. Both GINA-3D [ 48 ] and LRM [ 18 ] translate an image into a triplane representation based on a visual transformer. Then, a downstream generator can generate a new view of the 3D shape with the triplane representation as input. Such representations focus on the details of 3D vision rather than semantic understanding. 3729 2.3. Multi-Modal 3D Representation Learning 

This line of methods aims to establish unified representa-tions across images, text, and 3D visuals. ULIP [ 64 ] ini-tially proposed a multimodal alignment framework based on contrastive learning [ 21 ], using point clouds, although it was limited by small-scale datasets. Following ULIP’s framework, with the release of the large-scale dataset Ob-javerse [ 10 ], works such as OpenShape [ 30 ], Uni3D [ 72 ], and ULIP-2 [ 65 ] explored scalable 3D representation pre-training. However, these efforts focused on point clouds as the sole medium for 3D perception, overlooking the potential of multi-view images, which are abundant in visual details. In this work, we aim to combine point clouds and multi-view images to propose a new multi-modal 3D representation learning framework. 

# 3. Method 

We propose OpenView , an innovative approach for learning a unified representation across text, 2D images, and 3D visuals. Our proposed fusion framework for multi-view images and point clouds is detailed in Section 3.1 and illustrated in Figure 2. The model includes a cross-modal fusion encoder, as described in Section 3.2. The multi-modal alignment training objective is presented in Section 3.3. Finally, we explored a progressive hard training strategy for OpenView in Section 3.4. 

3.1. MVI-PC Fusion Framework 

Objective. Given a 3D shape or scene in the form of 

(P, M ) as input, where M denotes a set of multi-view im-ages {Ii}mi=1 and P ∈ Rg×6 denotes the point cloud, the model outputs a 3D representation eV ∈ RdE , aligned with the image-text embedding space of OpenCLIP [ 7]. Here, Ii

denotes the i-th image, m is the total number of multi-view images, dE is the latent dimension of the model, g is the number of points, and each point has x, y, z coordinates and RGB color. 

Components. The fusion model comprises three key components: an image encoder, a point cloud encoder, and a fusion encoder, as depicted in Figure 2. The framework is based on advanced embedding-based modeling, where embeddings are extracted from multi-view images and point clouds, allowing cross-modal fusion based on the attention mechanism. The image encoder independently extracts fea-tures {eIi }mi=1 ∈ RdE from m images of different views. The point cloud encoder extracts features {eP }ki=1 ∈ RdE from the point cloud. The cross-modal fusion encoder takes these features as input and outputs a embedding eV ∈ RdE as the 3D semantic representation. 

Image Encoder. The image encoder utilizes the prevalent transformer-based ViT [ 12 ] network. The image is divided into multiple 16 × 16 patches. Each patch is mapped as an embedding by a fully connected layer, added with a po-sitional embedding. With an extra [cls] embedding, these embeddings interact with a multi-layer transformer. In this process, each image is extracted into only one semantic em-bedding, i.e., the [cls] embedding of the last layer is output. The motivation behind this is: if all embeddings are out-putted to the subsequent fusion encoder, the GPU memory for inference will be extremely high. The batch size will need to be reduced, which negatively impacts downstream contrastive learning [43]. 

Point Cloud Encoder. The point cloud encoder uses the network structure of Point-bert [ 70 ]. The point cloud encoder divides the point cloud P ∈ Rg×6 into k sub-point clouds and then extracts each sub-point cloud as a patch embedding using a mini-PointNet [ 40 ]. Later, as in ViT [ 12 ], the positional embedding is added. With an additional [cls] embedding, these embeddings are interactively inferenced in a multi-layer transformer. 

Cross-modal Fusion Encoder. It is necessary to design a cross-modal fusion encoder to fuse the embeddings {ePi }ki=1 

from the point cloud modality and the embeddings {eIi }mi=1 

from the image modality. The cross-modal fusion encoder is designed as a variant of the transformer [ 54 ] structure. In particularly, two special tokens are set to aggregate the global information. The point-cloud modality utilizes the [cls] token output by the point cloud encoder, while the image modality takes the average feature of the image embeddings 

{eI }mi=1 as the special token. Furthermore, to modeling the intra-modal dependencies and inter-modal correlations between the two modalities, intra-modal and inter-modal attention mechanisms are designed, which will be described in detail in Section 3.2. 

Sequence-Independent Modeling. Since multi-view images are typically unordered, the output should ideally remain consistent regardless of the order of the input im-ages. For this reason, we did not add additional positional embedding in the subsequent cross-modal encoder for image embeddings. Furthermore, to allow the cross-modal fusion encoder to distinguish and accommodate the two modalities, before feeding into the fusion encoder, each feature is added with a learnable type embedding tI , t P ∈ RdE . As a result, OpenView can adapt to any number of multi-view images, and the output remains consistent regardless of the input order of the multi-view images. 

Parameter Initialization. The cross-modal fusion en-coder utilizes random initialization based on a uniform dis-tribution. The point cloud encoder is initialized with the pre-trained OpenShape [ 30 ] model. The image encoder is initialized with the pre-trained OpenCLIP [ 7 ]. This strategy yields two critical benefits: (a) it effectively leverages prior knowledge from large-scale pre-trained models, reducing training costs; and (b) it paves the way for aligning the em-bedding space of 3D data with the text-image embedding 3730 …

3.3. Cross-Modal Training Objective 

Motivation. This study aims to align the embedding space of 3D data with the text-image embedding space of OpenCLIP [7 ]. To achieve this, contrastive learning [ 43 ] is a prevalent paradigm for cross-modal representation learning. Previous 3731 works [ 30 , 64 , 65 , 72 ] have used contrastive learning to set images and text as alignment targets for 3D representations. However, a notable drawback of prior works is the random sampling of one view from the multi-view images as the alignment target. Some views of a 3D shape can be mislead-ing, for example, a wall lamp may appear like a frying pan from above. This can introduce unnecessary noise during alignment training. Therefore, unlike previous studies, we remove the image modality from the alignment targets. 3.4. Progressive Hard Learning 

Motivation. In comparison to prior work, we have intro-duced the image modality to enhance 3D understanding. However, 3D representation models may focus on a few easy-to-understand views of images, but give up a global 3D understanding. This limits a more comprehensive and deeper understanding. Inspired by curriculum learning [ 51 ], we propose a training strategy that employs a more challeng-ing training mode. This mode forces the 3D representation model to learn a more comprehensive correlation between the point cloud and the images. Specifically, in the hard training mode, partial easy-to-understand multi-view images are removed. The criteria for determining whether an image is easy-to-understand are based on the text description T of the 3D data. Multi-view images are sorted based on cosine similarity between image features and text features. To enable batch training in the hard mode, it is designed to remove a progressive proportion of input images for each training sample, rather than setting a threshold. For each epoch, 10% of the multi-view image is reduced until 50% of the multi-view image remains. 

# 4. Experiments 

4.1. Datasets 

We utilize the six datasets following [ 30 , 72 ]. The ensem-ble training datasets include Objaverse [10], ShapeNetCore [ 2], ABO [ 9], and 3D-Future [ 14 ]. The three evaluation benchmarks are Omniobject3D [ 61 ], ModelNet40 [ 62 ] and Objaverse-LVIS [ 10 ]. Each 3D model was sampled into a point cloud of 10,000 colored points and a set of 10 images captured from different viewpoints. • Objaverse dataset [ 10 ] comprises 798.8k 3D models. Note that Objaverse-LVIS is the evaluation subset of 46.8k 3D models across 1,156 categories that are re-moved during the training. • ShapeNetCore dataset [ 2] comprises 52.5k human-verified CAD 3D models across 55 categories. • ABO dataset [ 9] comprises 8.0k real-world 3D models derived from Amazon.com products. • 3D-Future dataset [ 14 ] comprises 16.6k 3D furniture models across 34 categories. • ModelNet40 test dataset [ 62 ] comprises 2.4k synthetic CAD 3D models across 40 categories. • Omniobject3D test dataset [ 61 ] comprises 6.0k real-world 3D models across 190 categories, with text de-scriptions annotated by human experts. 

Textual descriptions in training. Liu et al. [ 30 ] have used BLIP [ 24 ] and Azure Cognitive Services to generate textual descriptions for the ensemble training datasets. This results in two unique textual descriptions for each 3D model, which are used during the training phase. Specifically, for each training iteration, one of the two descriptions is ran-domly selected to serve as the target text. 

Manual text description in testing. In the cross-modal retrieval task, two test subsets of 1,000 3D models were sampled from Objaverse-LVIS [ 10 ] and Omniobject3D [ 61 ]. Furthermore, to ensure the quality of the evaluation, we man-ually annotated text descriptions for the subset of Objaverse-LVIS. In the zero-shot classification task, to be fair, we fol-low the template-based prompt engineering technique from ULIP [64] and OpenShape [30]. 

4.2. Implementation Details 

Training. Notably, the evaluation subset Objaverse-LVIS has been excluded from the training. The pre-trained image encoder and text encoder from OpenCLIP are frozen in train-ing due to their robust generalisation and large parameters. OpenView is trained on 8 Tesla V100 GPUs (32G) with a batch size of 64, a learning rate of 1e-7, and 10 epochs, taking 24 hours. The optimizer is AdamW [ 35 ] and utilizes the cosine schedule for the learning rate. 

Hyperparameter. The latent dimension dE , d F are set as 

1280 and 4096 . The layers of the cross-modal encoder and the point cloud encoder are set to 6 and 12, respectively. The image encoder and the text encoder utilises the ViT-bigG-14 model from OpenCLIP [7].

4.3. Zero-Shot Classification 

In zero-shot classification task, the evaluation is conducted on three benchmarks: Objaverse-LVIS [ 10 ], ModelNet40 [ 62 ], Omniobject3D [ 61 ]. The zero-shot classification is based on the cosine similarity between the 3D representation and the template-based prompt text representation. For ex-ample, the template ”a 3D model of {} ”, which will be filled with category names. The text encoder is used to obtain multiple template representations and perform averaging for classification. To be fair, we use the same template-based prompt as previous work [30, 64, 72]. Table 1 shows the results. It is evident that OpenView outperforms previous work on all three benchmarks. In particular, on the Objaverse-LVIS dataset, which is of high quality and has a large number of categories, OpenView achieves a new level of classification accuracy, increasing the Top-1, Top-3, and Top-5 accuracy to 52.7%, 74.6%, and 81.1%, respectively. This indicates a more comprehensive understanding of the real-world 3D domain. Furthermore, substituting the cross-modal encoder with a base transformer resulted in an 14.5% reduction in Top-1 accuracy, which re-flects the effectiveness of the cross-modal encoder proposed in this paper. On the ModelNet40 dataset, the advantage of OpenView is not significant. We analyze the reason is that the ModelNet40 dataset is a CAD synthetic 3D model with no color and no texture details, and thus the information benefit brought by the multi-view images is not substantial.

4.5. Ablation Study 

Input of multi-view images and point clouds. The results presented in Table 3 demonstrate that removing the multi-view images or the point clouds data resulted in a notable decline in the model’s performance. Furthermore, removing the multi-view image led to a more pronounced reduction in performance, with > 10% decreases in both the Top-1 classification and R@1 retrieval metrics. These findings substantiate the visual information benefits that multi-view images provide to the model. 

Fusion encoder. The fusion encoder has been replaced with the base transformer [ 54 ]. This has resulted in a 13.6% decrease in Top-1 and a 16.2% decrease in R@1. This comparison illustrates the pivotal role of the cross-modal fusion encoder that we developed for OpenView. 

Sequence-independent modeling. The sequence-independent modelling introduced in 3.1 is removed and positional embedding [ 23 ] is added. This variation results in a reduction in model performance, which is primarily due to the fact that multi-view images are typically unordered. 

Inter-modal and intra-modal attention. We remove inter-modal and intra-modal attention respectively to test their effectiveness. The model performance all decreased, demonstrating the necessity of modeling inter-modal rela-tionships and intra-modal dependencies. 

Number of multi-view images. In the majority of exper-iments in this paper, the number of multi-view images is 10. As the number of images decreases, there is a slight decline in the model performance, indicating that OpenView is not sensitive to the number of multi-view images. 

Progressive hard learning. To enable the model to focus on global features, we developed progressive hard learning introduced in 3.4. By gradually reducing the number of easy-to-understand images in training, the model’s ability to understand point clouds and ambiguous images is enhanced.

# 6. Discussion 

Does adding multi-view images to 3D representation in-troduce complexity in data collection? Multi-view images are one of the common data formats for 3D perception. For scenarios such as 3D games, e-commerce products and vir-tual reality, the 3D models are always visualised for the user, which makes it straightforward to collect multi-view images. Even in the autonomous driving scenarios, Tesla is explicitly based on the technical route of multi-view images [6]. 

Does the ambiguity of some images affect the under-standing of the model? One example of ambiguity is that a wall lamp may appear like a frying pan from above. In order to mitigate the effects of ambiguity, we have developed a pro-gressive hard learning strategy to enhance the understanding of these ambiguous images. 

Why not utilize a large language model [ 28 , 36 ] as the backbone? Instead of stacking large-scale model parameters and training data, our research focuses on exploring a fusion framework for multi-view images and point clouds. Our framework and idea can be leveraged by future work to incorporate 3D modalities into large language models. 

Does the addition of multi-view images have an impact on the robustness of the model? In contrast to previous work [ 30 , 64 , 65 , 72 ] in the field of 3D semantic representa-tion, our method adds the input of multi-view images. How-ever, neural networks are vulnerable to adversarial attacks [1]. For the image modality, researchers have proposed many adversarial attack [ 26 ] and defense [ 74 ] methods. Thus, it is necessary to explore the effect of adding multi-view images on model robustness in future work. 

# 7. Conclusion 

This work presents OpenView, an innovative framework for learning unified multi-modal 3D representations by fusing point clouds and multi-view images. Experiments indicate that OpenView outperforms the state-of-the-art in text-based retrieval and zero-shot classification tasks. Furthermore, OpenView aligns with the image-text embedding space of CLIP, thereby enabling a wide range of cross-modality appli-cations, such as 3D retrieval, 3D captioning and hierarchical clustering for database management, highlighting its versa-tility in the field of 3D representation learning. 3735 References
